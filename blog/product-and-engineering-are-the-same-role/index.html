THIS IS A PREVIEW POST THAT IS NOT YET FULLY PUBLISHED

2024-04-18 by Collin Green

Stop splitting up product and engineering

Hot take: "product" and "product engineering" are the same role and should be done by the same people. Nearly everything about the two stereotypical roles are the same and introducing a separation between them with folks that can only do part of the job on each side is making your product worse and is slowing you down.

There is a cargo-cult-like, blind acceptance of having separate departments for product and product engineering but it doesn't make any sense and it actively causes harm by preventing the right people from having all the context they need to build something great. Some companies need true technical engineering work (usually high scale backend work and things like site reliability) but product engineering belongs with product (and, if necessary, not with other engineering) since it is actually the same role in every way that matters.

Stop cutting your brain in half

Product engineering requires a lot of knowledge and context in order to make something great. This includes hands-on experience with the customer pain you are trying to solve, a deep understanding of what your current product does (including how it works), and a broad base of knowledge about what your options are. People need all of these at the same time in order to make something great; you can't only talk to customers or only play with code and do this job effectively.

Intentionally separating this across multiple people is madness.

You don't need all this overhead

If you think about the day-to-day time spent in traditional product and engineering setups it is mostly wasted overhead trying to bridge this artificial gap; endless meetings, task trackers, documents, diagrams, emails, and chats are used to try to share the necessary understanding between separate people from separate departments. If this split didn't exist, most of these time killers wouldn't either! Imagine how much time people would have to actually get real work done if they didn't spend most of their time (and more importantly, innovation) just trying to get on the same page.

More significantly than the wasted time, splitting this across multiple people creates a severe bottleneck for even coming up with solutions to real problems. If everything has to be documented and communicated between people with different silos of knowledge and ability it becomes very difficult to do anything significant.

The worst case of this I have witnessed was a company unable to implement even a basic version of search because the product team couldn't understand the costs of a thorough version and the engineering team couldn't counter with a simple version that did a good job of addressing the underlying need. This consumed many many hours from many many people and the end result was the worst case; despite a clear need for search they couldn't come up with a viable way forward so they did nothing.

Never split the difference

Even when they aren't at a complete impasse, when the people coming up with the ideas and the people building the ideas are different there will always be tradeoffs made between them, usually ending in a mediocre middle ground.

When the two sides don't have a great understanding of each other's domain they end up making misinformed compromises and trying to 'work around' each other - product starts avoiding ambitious goals because they blindly guess that the work will be 'hard' and engineering starts designing product themselves without actually understanding all the customers or their needs.

By putting the two sides at odds with each other you create an environment of both waste and imperfect compromise and a vast amount of time and energy is spent trying to keep people on the same page.

You need the same mindset in order to succeed

Efficiency and effectiveness aside, the actual skills and mindset are the same as well. Building a great codebase that is easy to learn, scale, and maintain requires exactly the same kind of thinking as designing a great user experience that is easy to master and can expand in functionality without devolving into a tangled mess.

This starts with the ability to destructure big problems into combinations of smaller, more tractable ones. Building with clear, modular ideas is critical to both good software architecture and product design. This also hinges on the self control to follow the decisions and work you've made in the past and avoid requirements that aren't well supported by them. When you eventually must support something new, you need the discipline to rewrite the underlying systems so they really do support it (not just tack something on poorly with a hack) and the broad and deep knowledge of your product and code so that you can truly understand the ramifications of such changes.

To be clear this is hard; this is what the craft of software engineering / product management truly is and this is what separates it from merely 'programming'. Doing this well is what makes teams speed up over time instead of slow down and it is much easier if the person coming up with the UX patterns initially is also the person who needs to choose the right abstractions to build and re-use those patterns in the code.

You need to feel the pain of decisions on both ends

Making software is weird and it really does have this phenomenon where some people or teams seem to go 10 times faster than others, often without a clear smoking gun to explain it. There are many things that go into this, but most of the 10x or 0.1x is the accumulation of many tiny decisions over time - either death by a thousand cuts or the cumulative leverage of a bunch of accretive decisions.

This can mean relatively big things like system architecture and data design but it often is seemingly minor things like product changes that feel small but introduce complexity to the code or rigidly enforcing code patterns that end up in slower performance or stripping the delight from the user experience.

I've personally watched both of these happen many times on both sides -- I've seen weeks of engineering time burned making low-value components able to support a bunch of slightly different layout variations because of wasteful product requirements and I've witnessed really weird UX concessions because bad engineering abstractions made it too hard to add buttons to the right place (and bad product+engineering discipline didn't go rewrite the underlying components and systems so it could support the desired outcome).

These decisions need to be made by the same people and those people need to deeply understand both sides of the push-and-pull.

Combine product and product-engineering for maximum gains

From this perspective, your product managers ARE your product engineers so make sure they can do the whole job! It takes the same customer knowledge and product knowledge in order to make good decisions and avoid bad ones. In a software company focused on finding product market fit the two jobs are almost identical - the skills required, the attitude, the day-to-day activities, and even the way the roles change as they become more senior. Significantly, both rely on deep understanding and strong systematic thinking and the same kinds of decisions lead to '10x' or '0.1x' outputs. Separating the two jobs limits the potential in either one; you can't create an excellent product without deeply understanding both the problem your customers have and what is available to you to solve them.

NOTE! This advice doesn't apply equally to all fields or all company types - this is explicitly about startup-like, innovative, software companies with small teams; I have no idea how to run 100,000 person teams or build bridges or put satellites in space or demolish mountains or anything else so use your brain to decide what applies and what doesn't.

For giant, slow companies it might even make sense to hire people who can only do one role or the other (it might even be the only option) but in a mid-tech company focused on speed or excellence it is a mistake to have product people that aren't engineers or engineers that can't think about product because it is extremely hard to separate the two ideas and still make something good. Awesome products come from deeply understanding the problem your customers have and no amount of work tickets, kanban cards, or standup meetings are going to convey the necessary depth or passion.
